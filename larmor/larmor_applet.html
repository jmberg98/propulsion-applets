Larmor Motion

Plasma Physics

Thermochemistry and compressible flow govern the operating fundamentals of chemical rocket engines. Likewise, electromagnetism and plasma physics govern the operating fundamentals of electric thrusters. We start by exploring the non-intuitive behavior of charged particles in a uniform magnetic field.

Guiding Question

What path/trajectory will a charged particle follow if it travels entirely perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field?

Perpendicular Motion
Cyclotron & Larmor Motion

When a particle travels perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field, it travels circularly, undergoing cyclotron or Larmor motion. The radius of this circle is called the Larmor radius, and the angular frequency of the motion is called the cyclotron frequency. A particle in Larmor motion is said to be "magnetized" (more on this in the magnetic mirror applet!).

Conceptual Questions
What does a magnetic field do to a charged particle? How is the particle accelerated? Does the magnetic field speed up or slow down the charged particle?
A magnetic field exerts no force on a charged particle if the particle has zero velocity, or if the particle has nonzero velocity but is traveling entirely tangent to the magnetic field lines. If instead the charged particle has a nonzero velocity component perpendicular to $\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}$, the magnetic force provides centripetal acceleration to the particle. Recall that centripetal acceleration doesn't speed up or slow down a body, but rather changes its direction. In fact, the particle undergoes circular motion around the field line! Furthermore, because the particle can be positively or negatively charged, the direction of rotation can either be clockwise or counter-clockwise.
The ionization chambers of electric propulsion devices generally contain three species: neutral propellant atoms, positively charged ions, and negatively charged electrons. How are each of these three species affected by a magnetic field?
Neutral propellant atoms are (by definition) not charged, so they are entirely unaffected by a magnetic field. Positively charged ions and negatively charged electrons are both magnetized by magnetic field lines and travel in a helical trajectory. They will wind in opposite directions, given that they are oppositely charged. More interestingly, the mass of the ion is 3–5 orders of magnitude larger than the electron. The ion will therefore have a much larger Larmor radius and smaller cyclotron frequency, while the electron will have a much smaller Larmor radius and greater cyclotron frequency.
Larmor Radius

The Larmor radius is the radius of a particle's circular orbit in a uniform magnetic field. The greater the mass of the particle, the larger the radius. Conversely, the greater the charge and magnetic field strength, the smaller the radius.

$$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L} = \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}}{|\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}} } $$
Show derivation
Setup — particle parameters Apply the Lorentz force to a particle of mass $m$ and charge $q$ traveling at total speed $|\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v}|$ at pitch angle $\theta$ to $\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}$, decomposed into perpendicular and parallel components: $$ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp} = |\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v}|\cos\theta \qquad \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel} = |\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v}|\sin\theta $$
Lorentz Force & Newton's Second Law $$ \vec{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) \qquad \vec{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}} = \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\vec{a} $$ For $\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{E}} = 0$ with $\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} = B\hat{z}$ $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}}}{dt} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) $$
The magnetic force is always perpendicular to the particle's velocity, so it provides centripetal accleration.
Equate magnetic force to centripetal force $$ \underbrace{|\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}_{\text{magnetic}} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}^2}{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L}}}_{\text{centripetal}} $$ Solve for $r_L$ $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L} = \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}}{|\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}} } $$
A heavier ion gyrates with a larger radius than a light electron at the same speed. A stronger field or larger charge tightens the orbit.
Cyclotron Frequency

The cyclotron frequency is the angular frequency of a particle's circular orbit in a uniform magnetic field. The greater the mass of the particle, the lesser the frequency. Conversely, the greater the charge and magnetic field strength, the greater the frequency.

$$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\omega_c} = \frac{|\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}} } $$
Show derivation
Starting point — centripetal balance (from Larmor radius) $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}^2}{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L}} = |\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B} $$ Centripetal motion has angular velocity $\omega_c = v_\perp / r_L$ $$ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp} = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\omega_c}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L} $$ Substitute into the centripetal balance $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\omega_c}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L})^2}{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L}} = |\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\omega_c}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L})\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B} $$ $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\omega_c}\,\cancel{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L}} = |\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}\,\cancel{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{r_L}} $$ Solve for $\omega_c$ $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\omega_c} = \frac{|\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}} } $$
Linear frequency & period $$ f_c = \frac{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\omega_c}}{2\pi} = \frac{|\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}{2\pi\,\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}} \qquad T_c = \frac{1}{f_c} = \frac{2\pi\,\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}{|\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}|\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}} $$
