Delta-V & The Ideal Rocket Equation

Mission Analysis

The purpose of a spacecraft propulsion system is to deliver a change in velocity to the vehicle of interest. Orbital maneuvers require a certain change in velocity to be executed. Likewise, the effect of rarefied drag in low orbit and the loss of velocity from fighting against gravity necessitate a certain change in velocity to maintain the spacecraft's trajectory. This "certain change in velocity" is quantified as "Delta-V". Orbital mechanics dictates the required Delta-V for a mission given the necessary maneuvers and Delta-V losses to gravity and drag. The sum is the required Delta-V budget for the mission. The ideal rocket equation tells us that the available Delta-V budget for the mission is a function of the effective exhaust velocity, or specific impulse, of the propulsion system, the vehicle's initial (wet) mass, and the vehicle's final (dry) mass. However, it is not immediately obvious how these parameters affect the Delta-V budget, nor is it immediately obvious how Delta-V budgets translate to real missions. To better understand Delta-V, and how the ideal rocket equation affects mission design, let's explore our solar system.

Clarifying Points
What is aerobraking? Why don't all destinations in the Solar System have it available?
Aerobraking involves using a planet's atmosphere to slow down a spacecraft via drag, giving the spacecraft the ability to reach the destination without spending any Delta-V. This requires the destination to have sufficient atmosphere to make aerobraking possible. Additionally, aerobraking only helps a spacecraft during arrival, not when a spacecraft is departing from a planetary body. Therefore, takeoff from enormous bodies with high escape velocities cannot benefit from aerobraking.
How were the Delta-V values decided for the solar system map? Don't different maneuvers have different Delta-V costs?
For simplicity, all maneuvers are done without gravity assists, which would reduce the required Delta-V for a given destination. All transfers are assumed to be Hohmann transfers with burns performed at the periapses. These maneuvers are only possible with highly impulsive propulsion devices, which have low specific impulse. Devices with greater mass efficiency, however, cannot produce these high impulse burns, and must use low thrust spiral maneuvers. While these spiral maneuvers have higher Delta-V costs, the Delta-V budget of these devices far exceeds those of high impulse devices.
Conceptual Questions
The difference between the dry and wet mass of the vehicle is the propellant mass. If a mission requires higher Delta-V, why don't we simply add more propellant mass?
Adding more propellant mass is a valid way to increase the Delta-V budget. It must be noted that adding propellant mass means that the spacecraft propulsion system must now accelerate the mass of the additional propellant as well. Because of this, adding propellant mass has diminishing returns on increasing Delta-V. Additionally, additional propellant mass can require additional structural mass. While specific impulse has the strongest effect on increasing Delta-V, refueling a spacecraft in orbit can also be an effective method of increasing Delta-V. Accomplishing this, however, would require multiple launches and orbital rendezvous to replenish the propellant of the spacecraft. This is by no means trivial, but could be revolutionary for high Delta-V, high payload missions.
Greater specific impulse means that our propulsion system is more mass efficient. How does this affect our Delta-V budget?
Greater mass efficiency lessens the need to carry additional propellant to achieve a given Delta-V. Increases in specific impulse, or effective exhaust velocity, results in a linear increase in Delta-V. To reach far destinations, or to reach a destination with less propellant and more payload, greater specific impulses are required. This is the motivation for electric propulsion, which is further explored below the applet.

Specific Impulse

Recall that specific impulse is a measure of how efficiently propellant mass is used to deliver impulse to our spacecraft. Specific impulse and effective exhaust velocity are often used interchangeably, as they are directly proportional to each other. It is important to note the units, m/s and s, which indicate whether exhaust velocity or specific impulse is being referenced.

Show derivation
Total impulse $$ \begin{aligned} \text{Impulse} &= \int \textcolor{#FF4400}{F_N}\,dt = \int \textcolor{#FF4400}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}\,dt = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}\int \textcolor{#FF4400}{\dot{m}}\,dt = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}\,\textcolor{#FF4400}{m_{\mathrm{prop}}} \end{aligned} $$ Normalize by propellant mass $$ \frac{\text{Impulse}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m_{\mathrm{prop}}}} = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c} $$ Specific impulse $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{I_{sp}} = \frac{\text{Impulse}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m_{\mathrm{prop}}}\,g_0} = \frac{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}}{g_0} } $$
Motivation for Electric Propulsion

Increasing effective exhaust velocity $c$, or specific impulse $I_{sp}$, is clearly the greatest driver for achieving high $\Delta V$ or meeting a $\Delta V$ specification using less propellant. Assume we have a chemical engine that uses liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen as propellants, one of the most energetic reactions available to us. Assume that combustion is 100% efficient and that the engine nozzle is perfectly expanded such that exit pressure is 0 Pascals. These assumptions are intentionally optimistic. In making them, we derive the upper limit of the specific impulse for chemical engines, which is around 500 seconds. If we want to increase specific impulse further, we must decouple the energy source of the propulsion system from the propellant. If we instead apply a voltage to a charged propellant, we can clearly achieve much higher specific impulses, limited only by our choice of mass flow rate and power. Electric propulsion aims to achieve much higher specific impulses than available by traditional chemical engines.

Energy per unit mass $$ \textcolor{#C060FF}{E}=\frac{1}{2}\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}^2 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}=\sqrt{2\textcolor{#C060FF}{E}} } $$ For chemical rockets, the power is coupled to the propellant

Suppose we use LH$_2$ & LOX combustion

$$ 2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O $$ The reaction has energy per unit mass $$ \textcolor{#C060FF}{E} = 1.34\times10^7\ \mathrm{J/kg} $$ $$ \Rightarrow\quad \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c} = 4927\ \mathrm{m/s} \qquad\text{or}\qquad \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{I_{sp}} = 502\ \mathrm{s} $$ For electric rockets, however, power and propellant are decoupled from each other

$c$ can be made higher by applying a large amount of voltage to a small amount of gas.
