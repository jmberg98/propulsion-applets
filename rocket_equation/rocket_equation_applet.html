Delta-V & The Ideal Rocket Equation

In-Space

The purpose of a spacecraft propulsion system is to deliver a change in velocity to the vehicle of interest. Orbital manuevers require a certain change in velocity to be executed. Likewise, the effect of rareified drag in low orbit and the loss of velocity from fighting against gravity necessitate a certain charge in velocity to maintain the spacecraft's trajectory. This "certain change in velocity" is quantified as "Delta-V." Orbital mechanics dictates the required Delta-V for a mission given the necessary manuevers and Delta-V losses to gravity and drag. The sum is the requireed Delta-V budget for the mission. The ideal rocket equation tells us that the available Delta-V budget for the mission is a function of the effective exhaust velocity, or specfic impulse, of the propulsion system, the vehicle's initial (wet) mass, and the vehicle's final (dry) mass. However, it is not immediately obvious how these parameters affect the Delta-V budget, nor is it immediately obvious how Delta-V budgets translate to real missions. To better understand Delta-V, and how the ideal rocket equation affects mission design, let's explore our solar system.

Clarifying Points
What is aerobraking? Why don't all destinations in the Solar System have it available?
Aerobraking involves using a planet's atmosphere to slow down a spacecraft via drag, giving the spacecraft the ability to reach the destination without spending any Deta-V. This requires the destination to have sufficient atmosphere to make aerobraking possible. Additionally, aerobraking only helps a spacecraft during arrival, not when a spacecraft is departing from a planetary body. Therefore, takeoff from enormous bodies with high escape velocities cannot benefit from aerobraking.
How were the Delta-V values decided for the solar system map? Don't different manuevers have different Delta-V costs?
For simplicity, all manuevers are done without gravity assists, which would reduce the required Delta-V for a given destination. All transfers are assumed to be Hohmann transfers with burns performed at the periapses. These manuevers are only possible with highly impulsive propulsion devices, which have low specific impulse. Devices with greater mass efficiency, however, cannot produce these high impulse burns, and must use low thrust spiral manuevers. While these spiral maneuvers have higher Delta-V costs, the Delta-V budget of these devices far exceeds those of high impulse devices.
Conceptual Questions
The difference between the dry and wet mass of the vehicle is the propellant mass. If a mission requires higher Delta V, why don't we simply add more propellant mass?
Adding more propellant mass is a valid way to increase the Delta-V budget. It must be noted that adding propellant mass means that the spacecraft propulsion system must now accelerate the mass of the additional propellant as well. Because of this, adding propellant mass has diminishing returns on increasing Delta-V. Additionally, additional propellant mass can require additional structural mass. While specific impulse has the strongest effect on increasing Delta-V, refueling a spacecraft in orbit can also be an effective method of increasing Delta-V. Accomplishing this, however, would require multiple launches and orbital rendezvous to replenish the propellant of the spacecraft. This is by no means trivial, but could be revolutionary for high Delta-V, high payload missions.
Greater specific impulse means that our propulsion system is more mass efficient. How does this affect our Delta-V budget?
Greater mass efficiency lessens the need to carry additional propellant to achieve a given Delta-V. Increases in specific impulse, or effective exhaust velocity, results in a linear increase in Delta-V. To reach far destinations, or to reach a destination with less propellant and more payload, greater specific impulses are required. This is the motivation for electric propulsion, which is further explored below the applet.

Motivation for Electric Propulsion

$c$ is clearly the greatest driver for achieving high $\Delta V$ or meeting a $\Delta V$ specification using less propellant.

Show derivation
Energy per unit mass $$ E=\frac{1}{2}\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}^2 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}=\sqrt{2E} } $$ For chemical rockets, the power is coupled to the propellant

Suppose we use LH$_2$ & LOX combustion

$$ 2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O $$ The reaction has energy per unit mass $$ E = 1.34\times10^7\ \mathrm{J/kg} $$ $$ \Rightarrow\quad \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c} = 4927\ \mathrm{m/s} \qquad\text{or}\qquad \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{I_{sp}} = 502\ \mathrm{s} $$ For electric rockets, however, power and propellant are decoupled from each other

$E$ can be higher by using a large amount of power to accelerate a small amount of propellant.
