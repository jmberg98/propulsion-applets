Gridded Ion Engine: Overview

Electric Propulsion

Before we look into the physics, it is important to acknowledge the history under which electrostatic thrusters were developed. Nearly all electric propulsion devices used today are electrostatic devices, meaning they use solely electric fields to accelerate charged particles for thrust. During the Cold War, the United States and Soviet Union began research on two such electrostatic thrusters: the gridded ion engine and the Hall-Effect thruster. While the United States began early theoretical research on the Hall-Effect thruster, it had plasma instabilities that dissuaded further development efforts. The United States would go on to focus on the development of the gridded ion engine, while the Soviet Union would continue to research the Hall-Effect thruster.

Gridded ion engines are highly efficient electric propulsion devices used on modern satellite platforms and several high profile missions. These ion engines have three characteristic features: an ionization chamber, accelerator grids, and a neutralizer. While each of these features varies from thruster to thruster, each variation serves the same purpose.

Ionization

The first stage of the gridded ion engine involves the ionization chamber, which ionizes neutral propellant atoms so that they can be propelled by the upcoming accelerator grids. Because ionization requires a certain amount of energy, ionization chambers are designed to prevent electrons from being immediately collected by the inside wall, which acts as an anode, so that it has as many opportunities to ionize neutral propellant particles as possible.

Acceleration

The second stage of the gridded ion engine provides the thrust of the engine via multiple accelerating grids. A voltage difference is applied between a screen grid and an accelerating grid, creating an electric field that accelerates an ion that happens to drift into the grid holes. However, there exists a limit on how many ions can be between the screen and accelerating grid: the more ions that exist there, the more incoming ions are repelled away.

Neutralization

The third stage of the gridded ion engine neutralizes the ion beam expelled from the accelerating grids by emitting electrons that join the beam, creating an overall neutral jet of ions and electrons: a plasma jet. If the ion beam were not neutralized, the spacecraft would be left negatively charged, since all the positive charge has left via the jet.

Circuit Diagrams

There are several circuits in gridded ion engines. Each circuit must be closed to prevent the spacecraft from accumulating charge. In a given circuit, the current out must equal the current in and the sum of voltage rises must equal the sum of voltage drops. See if you can identify each of the circuits above!

The Circuits
Circuit 1
The first circuit is between the electron-emitting “bombardment cathode” and the inside walls of the ion engine, which serve as the anode. When an electron is emitted from the bombardment cathode, it will travel throughout the chamber until it eventually touches the wall and is collected, completing the circuit. If an electron hits a neutral particle and ionizes it, the reaction results in a positively charged ion and two electrons. The newly added “secondary” electron can also be collected by the walls. In fact, the ion may hit the walls by chance, losing its charge and becoming neutral again.
Circuit 2
The second circuit is between the screen and accelerating grids toward the ion engine exit. The circuit creates a voltage difference between the grids, accelerating any ions that happen to drift in between the two. However, there is an ion jet, a form of current, leaving the grids, meaning the circuit isn’t quite closed (yet).
Circuit 3
The third circuit is between the neutralizer cathode, anode walls, and outgoing ion jet. The neutralizer cathode emits the collected electrons in the ionization chamber, expelling them toward the ion jet and completing Circuit 2.
Conceptual Questions
Gridded ion engines have very high specific impulses, in the order of several thousands of seconds, permitting them to be used on very long missions. However, missions are often more limited by their thruster components’ lifetimes rather than the amount of propellant available to them. What component is generally the most limiting to the lifetime of the thruster?
The accelerating grid is often the most lifetime-limiting component in a gridded ion engine. By the time ions reach the accelerating grid, they have reached their maximum speed. If they collide with the grid instead of passing through the gaps, they “sputter”, eroding the grid material. Additionally, ionization can occur past the accelerating grids. At this point, the newly created ion is moving slowly and is highly attracted to the negatively charged accelerator grid, causing erosion. Most gridded ion engines implement a “decelerator grid”, which is neutral. Ions that come back toward the accelerator grid thus hit the decelerator instead, extending the operating lifetime of the accelerator grid.
