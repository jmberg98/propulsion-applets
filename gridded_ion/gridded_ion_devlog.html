The section morph blends the perspective and orthographic projection matrices element-wise with weight b, and drives b *linearly* with progress. But an element-wise matrix blend is badly non-linear perceptually. The on-screen foreshortening is:

```

C(b) = (1−b)·worldZ / ((1−b)·d + b)

```

Because (1−b)·d dominates the denominator until b is within ~0.14% of 1, the view stays full perspective for essentially the entire animation and then snaps flat in the final instant:

b00.50.90.990.9991
C0.1730.1720.1700.1520.0730

The fix: remap the linear flatness progress f to the matrix-blend weight with a closed form that makes the *perspective-ness* fade linearly:

$$b(f) = \frac{d\,f}{(1-f) + d\,f}$$

At f=0.5, d=736 this gives b≈0.9986 — exactly the halfway-flat point. So the flatten now eases evenly across the whole morph instead of at the very end.

Question, does the E cross B drift make the electrons spin around the annulus?
Claude

Good physics question — and the short answer is: geometrically yes, but it's not the dominant mechanism in a gridded ion engine the way it is in a Hall thruster.

Why there *is* an azimuthal E×B drift

In our cross-section, both E and B lie in the meridional (r–z) plane:

  • B is poloidal — the ring-cusp arcs you see loop in the r–z plane, with no azimuthal component (axisymmetric).
  • E is also in-plane — radial at the wall sheaths, axial toward the grids.

Since v_drift = (E × B)/B² and both vectors are in the r–z plane, E × B points azimuthally — out of the page in the cross-section. So wherever there's appreciable B *and* a crossed E (i.e. right at the ring cusps and along the walls), electrons do drift around the axis — a closed loop circling each ring. In the 2D section view you'd never *see* it spinning; it's directed into/out of the screen.

But in a gridded (Kaufman / ring-cusp) engine it's a side effect, not the design

  • The chamber bulk is nearly field-free and quasineutral, so there's almost no B there and thus no meaningful E×B drift over most of the volume. The drift only lives in the thin cusp/wall regions.
  • The confinement the magnets are actually *for* is magnetic mirroring at the cusps — bouncing primary electrons back and forth to lengthen their path so they ionize more neutrals before being lost to the anode. The azimuthal E×B circulation around each ring contributes to that extended path, but it isn't the headline mechanism.

Where "electrons spinning around the annulus" *is* the whole point: Hall thrusters

A Hall thruster has an *annular* channel with a radial B and axial E — crossed so that E×B is purely azimuthal, driving a strong closed Hall current of electrons circulating around the annulus. That trapped azimuthal electron population is the defining feature of the device and is what ionizes the propellant and sustains the discharge. Our gridded ion thruster has a *cylindrical* discharge chamber and a DC ring-cusp discharge, so it doesn't run on that mechanism.

So: your intuition about the drift direction is correct (it's azimuthal, around the axis), but the "electrons spinning around the annulus as the main event" describes a Hall thruster — in this ring-cusp ion engine it's a localized cusp effect layered on top of magnetic-mirror confinement.

If it'd be useful, I could add a small note/annotation in the cross-section pointing out that E×B near the cusps is azimuthal (into the page) — though since it's out-of-plane it's genuinely hard to depict well in the axial section.
