Gridded Ion Engine I: Ionization

Electric Propulsion

In order for the ion engine to actually accelerate propellant to generate thrust, the propellant must be separated into positively and negatively charged particles, or ionized, in the ionization chamber. Neutral propellant particles are typically ionized by electron bombardment or radio-frequency (RF) wave emitters. Both deliver the required energy to separate an electron from a neutral particle, producing a positively charged ion that we can use for thrust!

Recall from the Ionization & Recombination Applet that electrons must have a certain amount of energy in order for a collision with a neutral particle to ionize it. Because of this, we want to maximize the time that the electron is in the ionization chamber. This way, an electron has more chances to ionize a neutral atom. Consider a utilization fraction $\mu_u$: the fraction of propellant injected into the ionization chamber that actually gets ionized. The greater this utilization fraction is, the more efficient the engine!

Magnetic Confinement
Conceptual Question
Only about 4–5% of the particles in the ionization chamber are ionized at any given time. Why are there relatively few ions in the ionization chamber compared to neutrals? How are high utilization fractions achieved in spite of this?
Most electron–neutral collisions don’t result in ionization, but rather an elastic collision. The electron keeps its energy and continues to travel about the chamber. As it does so, it can transfer energy with other electrons, which can then ionize the neutral propellant atoms. The magnetic field lines along the chamber walls help retain electrons, promoting ionization further. Neutral particles that drift toward the exit often bounce back into the chamber by colliding into either the screen or accelerating grid. Unlike ions that drift toward these grids, they aren’t guided by the high-strength electric field, and have a significant chance to return to the chamber to be ionized. All of these features help enable high utilization fractions, which tend to be 80% or greater for most gridded ion engines.
