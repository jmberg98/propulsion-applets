Gridded Ion Engine

Electric Propulsion

Before we look into the physics, it is important to acknowledge the history under which electrostatic thrusters were developed. Nearly all electric propulsion devices used today are electrostatic devices, meaning they use solely electric fields to accelerate charged particles for thrust. During the Cold War, the United States and Soviet Union began research on two such electrostatic thrusters: the gridded ion engine and the Hall-Effect thruster. While the United States began early theoretical research on the Hall-Effect thruster, it had plasma instabilities that dissuaded further development efforts. The United States would go on to focus on the development of the gridded ion engine, while the Soviet Union would continue to research the Hall-Effect thruster.

Gridded ion engines are highly efficient electric propulsion devices used on modern satellite platforms and several high profile missions. These ion engines have three characteristic features: an ionization chamber, accelerator grids, and a neutralizer. While each of these features varies from thruster to thruster, each variation serves the same purpose.
