Gridded Ion Engine II: Acceleration

Electric Propulsion

Now that most of the neutral propellant particles have been ionized, they are ready to be accelerated to produce useful thrust! A large voltage difference is applied over the short distance between the screen and accelerator grids, creating a strong electric field $E = V_T/d$. Much like chemical rockets, only so many particles can be accelerated at a time via the accelerator grid. This means that for a given gridded ion engine size, there is a limit to the amount of the thrust that can be generated. Let’s explore what the limit seems to be.

Stage 2 — Accelerating Grids

The second stage of the gridded ion engine is applying an electric field to particles that have drifted past the ionization chamber into the accelerating grids. Why are there (at least) two grids in gridded ion engines?

The more ions that pass through the accelerator grids, the greater our mass flow rate and thus our thrust. What causes the fundamental limit on the amount of ions that pass through the screen grid into the accelerator grid?

Child–Langmuir Space Charge
Conceptual Questions
If we have both positively charged ions and negatively charged electrons available to us, why do we always choose to accelerate the ions and not the electrons?
Recall that electrons have far less mass than ions, by three to four orders of magnitude. While they can be accelerated extremely fast, their minuscule mass means that the thrust an electron jet produces is near zero.
The larger $V_T/d$ (the electric field between the screen and accelerating grids) is, the higher thrust density we can achieve. Why can’t $V_T/d$ be arbitrarily high?
$V_T$ is determined by the desired mission and thus desired exhaust velocity or specific impulse, as related by $v_{ex} = \sqrt{2qV_T/m}$. Thus, to maximize $V_T/d$, we want to minimize $d$, the distance between the screen and accelerator grids. However, if $d$ is too small, then the risk of the screen–accelerator grid circuit shorting is high. This is especially true after vibrations during launch, as grids might shift, causing a short. Additionally, recall that $V_T/d$ is the electric field strength between the screen and accelerator grids. If $E$-field is too strong, then breakdown can occur: creating electrical arcs between the two grids, damaging them.
Child–Langmuir Law

In steady state, the ions streaming between the grids carry their own positive space charge, which bends the potential φ(x) set up by the grids. Solving Poisson’s equation for this self-consistent field gives the maximum current density the gap can transmit — the Child–Langmuir law.

Space-Charge-Limited Current Density $$ \boxed{\;\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j} = \frac{4}{9}\,\varepsilon_0 \sqrt{\frac{2q}{m}}\, \frac{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}^{3/2}}{d^{2}}\;} $$
Show Child–Langmuir derivation
Poisson’s Equation $$ \nabla^{2}\phi = \frac{d^{2}\phi}{dx^{2}} = -\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} $$ Current Density $$ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j} = \rho v \;\;\rightarrow\;\; \rho = \frac{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}}{v} $$ Conservation of Energy $$ \tfrac{1}{2}m v_0^{2} + q\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T} = \tfrac{1}{2}m v^{2} + q\phi $$ $$ \rightarrow\; v = \sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}\left(\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi\right)} \;\;\rightarrow\;\; \rho = \frac{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}}{\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}\left(\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi\right)}} $$ Substitute into Poisson’s Equation $$ \frac{d^{2}\phi}{dx^{2}} = -\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} = -\frac{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}}{\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}}\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi}} $$ Recall Chain + Power Rule $$ \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{d\phi}{dx}\right)^{2} = 2\,\frac{d\phi}{dx}\,\frac{d^{2}\phi}{dx^{2}} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{d\phi}{dx}\right)^{2} = \frac{-2\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}\,\frac{d\phi}{dx}}{\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}\,\sqrt{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi}} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \int_{0}^{\frac{d\phi}{dx}} d\!\left(\frac{d\phi}{dx}\right)^{2} = \frac{-2\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}}{\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}} \int_{V_T}^{\phi}\frac{d\phi'}{\sqrt{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi'}} $$

The field vanishes at the screen grid at space-charge saturation, so $\left.\tfrac{d\phi}{dx}\right|_{0} = 0$ with $\phi(0)=\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}$.

$$ \rightarrow\; \left(\frac{d\phi}{dx}\right)^{2} = \frac{-2\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}}{\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}}\,(-2)\left(\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi\right)^{1/2} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \frac{d\phi}{dx} = \sqrt{\frac{4\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}}{\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}}} \left(\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi\right)^{1/4} $$ Separate & Integrate $$ \int_{V_T}^{\phi}\frac{d\phi'}{\left(\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi'\right)^{1/4}} = \sqrt{\frac{4\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}}{\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}}}\int_{0}^{x}dx' $$ $$ \rightarrow\; -\frac{4}{3}\left(\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi\right)^{3/4}\Big|_{V_T}^{\phi} = \sqrt{\frac{4\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}}{\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}}}\;x $$ Square Both Sides $$ \frac{16}{9}\left(\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi\right)^{3/2} = \frac{4\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j}}{\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}}\,x^{2} $$ Rearrange for Current $$ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j} = \frac{4}{9}\,\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}\, \frac{\left(\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi\right)^{3/2}}{x^{2}} $$ Evaluate at $x=d,\;\phi=0$ $$ \boxed{\;\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{j} = \frac{4}{9}\,\varepsilon_0\sqrt{\tfrac{2q}{m}}\, \frac{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}^{3/2}}{d^{2}}\;} $$
Thrust Density

The thrust the grids can produce per unit area is the electric pressure pulling on the accelerator grid. Evaluating the Maxwell stress tensor at space-charge saturation caps the thrust density of a gridded ion engine.

Maximum Thrust Density $$ \boxed{\;\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}}{A} = \frac{8}{9}\,\varepsilon_0 \left(\frac{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}}{d}\right)^{2}\;} $$
Show thrust-density derivation
Maxwell Stress Tensor $$ T_{ij} = \sum \left(\varepsilon_0 E_i E_j - \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 \delta_{ij} E_k E_k\right) $$ Isolate $x$-component $$ T_{xx} = \varepsilon_0 E_x^{2} - \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0\left(E_x^{2}+E_y^{2}+E_z^{2}\right) $$ Electric Pressure $$ T = \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 \textcolor{#ef5350}{E}^{2} \;\;\rightarrow\;\; \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}}{A} = \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 \textcolor{#ef5350}{E}^{2} $$ Potential at Space-Charge Saturation $$ \frac{\left(\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}-\phi\right)^{3/2}}{x^{2}} = \frac{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}^{3/2}}{d^{2}} \;\;\rightarrow\;\; \left(1-\frac{\phi}{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}}\right)^{3/2} = \left(\frac{x}{d}\right)^{2} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; 1-\frac{\phi}{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}} = \left(\frac{x}{d}\right)^{4/3} \;\;\rightarrow\;\; \phi = \textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}\left(1-\left(\frac{x}{d}\right)^{4/3}\right) $$ Recall $E = -\,d\phi/dx$ $$ \textcolor{#ef5350}{E} = -\frac{d\phi}{dx} = \frac{4}{3}\left(\frac{x}{d}\right)^{1/3}\frac{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}}{d} $$ Substitute into Electric Pressure (at $x=d$) $$ \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}}{A} = \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 \textcolor{#ef5350}{E}^{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0\left(\frac{4}{3}\frac{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}}{d}\right)^{2} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \boxed{\;\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}}{A} = \frac{8}{9}\,\varepsilon_0 \left(\frac{\textcolor{#ef5350}{V_T}}{d}\right)^{2}\;} $$

The engine now expels a fast beam of positive ions — but that beam leaves the spacecraft charged. The final stage, neutralization, fixes that.
