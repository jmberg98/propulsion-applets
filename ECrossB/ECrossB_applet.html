E × B Motion

Plasma Physics

Magnetic fields do not speed up nor slow down charged particles, but instead change their direction of travel. If we apply an electric field perpendicular to the magnetic field, charged particles begin to drift in a direction perpendicular to both fields! Using the demos below, experiment with different field strengths with different charged particles to become more familiar with this “E cross B” drift.

Conceptual Question

If a particle starts stationary with a uniform electric field $E$ in the $\hat{\jmath}$ direction and a uniform magnetic field in the $\hat{k}$ direction, which way will the particle drift?

Ion vs. Electron Behavior

Let’s compare the behavior of ions and electrons. Ions are far more massive than electrons (by three to five orders of magnitude)! How might this affect their E cross B Drift? Assume that the electron and ion are in isolation of each other. They are shown together for visual purposes only.

Conceptual Question
What might happen if the initial vertical velocity ($+\hat{\jmath}$) of the charged particle in the demos is larger? Will the E cross B drift trajectory still be half circles, or something different?
If the initial vertical velocity is larger, then the magnetic force will grow in strength. Thus, the cyclotron motion “occurs faster” than the drift velocity. At the halfway point of the cyclotron motion, the particle begins heading backwards to complete the circle. If the drift velocity is equal to or greater than the initial vertical velocity, then the particle will stop or continue moving forward. However, with sufficiently large vertical velocity, the cyclotron motion plus the drift motion results in some backwards loop behavior. The path of the particle will then look a lot like a coil or strand of curly hair!
E × B Drift

The E Cross B Drift occurs due to the nature of acceleration of the electric field. At the halfway point of the cyclotron motion, the electric field prevents the trajectory from making a full circle, instead forcing the particle into starting the next circle. The greater the strength of the electric field, the greater the drift velocity. The greater the strength of the magnetic field, the smaller the drift velocity.

$$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d} = \frac{\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}^2} \;} $$
Show derivation
Apply the Lorentz force to a stationary particle with mass $m$ and charge $q$, where $\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}}, \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} \neq \vec{0}$. Lorentz Force & Newton's Second Law $$ \vec{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) \qquad \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}}}{dt} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) $$ Suppose $\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}(t) + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}$, where $\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}(t)$ is time-varying cyclotron motion about the magnetic field and $\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}$ is constant motion assumed perpendicular to the magnetic field. Decompose the velocity $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\right) = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\right)\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} $$ $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}}{dt} + 0 = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) + \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) $$ Recall the solution for cyclotron motion $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}}{dt} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) $$ $$ \cancel{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right)} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) + \cancel{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right)} $$ $$ 0 = \cancel{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) \;\Longrightarrow\; \textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} = 0 $$
Take the cross product of both sides with $\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}$ $$ \textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} + \left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right)\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} = 0 $$ Use the vector identity $(\vec{A}\times\vec{B})\times\vec{C} = \vec{B}(\vec{A}\cdot\vec{C}) - \vec{A}(\vec{B}\cdot\vec{C})$ $$ \textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} + \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\underbrace{\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\cdot\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right)}_{=\,0} - \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\left(\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\cdot\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) = 0 $$ $$ \textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} - \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}^2 = 0 $$ Solve for the drift velocity $$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d} = \frac{\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}^2} \;} $$
The drift is perpendicular to both fields, and is independent of the particle's mass and charge — ions and electrons drift together. A stronger electric field speeds up the drift; a stronger magnetic field slows it down.
