E × B Motion

Plasma Physics

To give a charged particle speed, we must apply an electric field over a distance, applying a voltage to the charged particle. Recall the common phrase “opposites attract, likes repel.” In doing so, we repel a positively charged particle away from our positively charged surface, creating thrust! Using the first demo, experiment with different voltages or electric field setups with different charged particles to see what exhaust speeds are possible!

Conceptual Question

Is it possible to exceed the specific impulse of a chemical rocket by accelerating a charged particle with an electric field?

Demo 1 — Electric Field Only
Crossed Fields & Drift

Magnetic fields do not speed up nor slow down charged particles, but instead change their direction of travel. If we apply an electric field perpendicular to the magnetic field, charged particles begin to drift in a direction perpendicular to both fields! Using the second and third demos, experiment with different field strengths with different charged particles to become more familiar with this “E cross B” drift.

Conceptual Question

Exhaust Velocity

The exhaust velocity of a charged particle with charge $q$ and mass $m$ after having an electric field of magnitude $E$ over a distance $d$, or equivalently a voltage difference $\Delta V$ or $\phi$ applied is:

$$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_e} = \sqrt{2\,\frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,\Delta \textcolor{#F5C518}{V}} \;} \qquad \boxed{\; \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_e} = \sqrt{2\,\frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|\,d} \;} $$
Show derivation
Via the Electric Field
Apply the Lorentz force to a stationary particle with mass $m$ and charge $q$. Lorentz Force & Newton's Second Law $$ \vec{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) \quad \vec{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}} = \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}}}{dt} $$ For $\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} = 0$ $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}}}{dt} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}}}{dt} = \frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} $$ For uniform, constant $\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}}$ $$ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v} = \frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|\,t \qquad x = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|\,t^2 $$ Use variables at desired $v_e$ & distance $d$ $$ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_e} = \frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|\,t_e $$ $$ d = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|\,t_e^2 \;\Longrightarrow\; t_e = \sqrt{2\,\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,d}{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|}} $$ Substitute $t_e$ $$ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_e} = \frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|\sqrt{2\,\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,d}{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|}} = \sqrt{2\,\frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|\,d} $$
Via the Voltage
Apply a potential difference (or voltage) to a stationary particle with mass $m$ and charge $q$. The exhaust velocity is measured when $V_f = 0$. Energy Balance $$ KE_i + \text{PE}_i = KE_f + \text{PE}_f $$ $$ \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_i}^{2}}_{=\,0} + \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\Delta \textcolor{#F5C518}{V} = \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_e}^{2} + \underbrace{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{\phi_f}}_{=\,0} $$ Solve for $v_e$ $$ \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\Delta \textcolor{#F5C518}{V} = \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_e}^{2} $$ $$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_e} = \sqrt{2\,\frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}\,\Delta \textcolor{#F5C518}{V}} \;} $$
The two forms are equivalent: the field and voltage are related by $\Delta \textcolor{#F5C518}{V} = |\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}|\,d$, so $|\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}| = \dfrac{\Delta \textcolor{#F5C518}{V}}{d}$.
Electric Field & Potential

The electric field and potential are related by a work-energy relation. For a given electric potential difference applied over a greater distance, there is a weaker electric field. Likewise, for a given electric potential difference applied over a smaller distance, there is a stronger electric field. Notice that the electric field lines point in the direction of decreasing electric potential. This is very similar to gravitational potential energy (think of a ball on a slope!). We will return to these potential gradients when examining magnetic mirrors.

$$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} = -\nabla\textcolor{#F5C518}{\phi} = -\frac{d\textcolor{#F5C518}{\phi}}{dx}\ \text{(in 1D)} \;} $$
Show derivation
Connect the electric field $\vec{E}$ and electric potential $\phi$ or $V$ by energy balance. Work done on charge = Change in electric potential energy $$ -\!\int \textcolor{#FF4400}{F_e}\,dx = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{V} $$ $$ -\!\int \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{E}\,dx = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{V} $$ Use the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus $$ -\cancel{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{E} = \cancel{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#F5C518}{V}}{dx} $$ Solve for $E$ $$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#F5C518}{E} = -\frac{d\textcolor{#F5C518}{V}}{dx} = -\frac{d\textcolor{#F5C518}{\phi}}{dx} \;} $$
E × B Drift

The E Cross B Drift behavior occurs due to the nature of acceleration of the electric field. At the halfway point of the cyclotron motion, the electric field prevents the trajectory from making a full circle, instead forcing the particle into starting the next circle. The greater the strength of the electric field, the greater the drift velocity. The greater the strength of the magnetic field, the smaller the drift velocity.

$$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d} = \frac{\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}^2} \;} $$
Show derivation
Apply the Lorentz force to a stationary particle with mass $m$ and charge $q$, where $\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}}, \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} \neq \vec{0}$. Lorentz Force & Newton's Second Law $$ \vec{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) \qquad \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}}}{dt} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) $$ Suppose $\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}(t) + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}$, where $\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}(t)$ is time-varying cyclotron motion about the magnetic field and $\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}$ is constant motion assumed perpendicular to the magnetic field. Decompose the velocity $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\right) = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\right)\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} $$ $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}}{dt} + 0 = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) + \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) $$ Recall the solution for cyclotron motion $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}}{dt} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) $$ $$ \cancel{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right)} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) + \cancel{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\!\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_L}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right)} $$ $$ 0 = \cancel{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) \;\Longrightarrow\; \textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} = 0 $$
Take the cross product of both sides with $\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}$ $$ \textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} + \left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right)\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} = 0 $$ Use the vector identity $(\vec{A}\times\vec{B})\times\vec{C} = \vec{B}(\vec{A}\cdot\vec{C}) - \vec{A}(\vec{B}\cdot\vec{C})$ $$ \textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} + \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\underbrace{\left(\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\cdot\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right)}_{=\,0} - \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\left(\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\cdot\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\right) = 0 $$ $$ \textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} - \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}^2 = 0 $$ Solve for the drift velocity $$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}_d} = \frac{\textcolor{#F5C518}{\vec{E}} \times \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}^2} \;} $$
The drift is perpendicular to both fields, and is independent of the particle's mass and charge — ions and electrons drift together. A stronger electric field speeds up the drift; a stronger magnetic field slows it down.
Conceptual Questions
Why are we exploring both electric and magnetic fields? If an electric field is all that is needed to accelerate a particle to very high velocities, why are electric propulsion devices complex?
Electric and magnetic fields allow us to manipulate the trajectories of charged particles in more ways than simply “forward” or “backward.” Recall that the inert gas propellant of electric propulsion devices is not itself ionized. It must be ionized by other means, requiring radio-frequency (RF) devices or emitters, before it can be accelerated by a plate.

Additionally, the positively charged exhaust jet must be neutralized! Why? Recall that charge, like mass and energy, must be conserved. If a lot of positive charge leaves the spacecraft, the spacecraft is left charged highly negative, which can disrupt electronics and be catastrophic. Moreover, if the positive charge is not neutralized, it will eventually accelerate back to the negatively charged spacecraft, canceling the thrust! These factors introduce engineering requirements of electric propulsion devices beyond an accelerating electric field.
What might happen if the initial vertical velocity ($+\hat{\jmath}$) of the charged particle in the demos is larger? Will the E cross B drift trajectory still be half circles, or something different?
If the initial vertical velocity is larger, then the magnetic force will grow in strength. Thus, the cyclotron motion “occurs faster” than the drift velocity. At the halfway point of the cyclotron motion, the particle begins heading backwards to complete the circle. If the drift velocity is equal to or greater than the initial vertical velocity, then the particle will stop or continue moving forward. However, with sufficiently large vertical velocity, the cyclotron motion plus the drift motion results in some backwards loop behavior. The path of the particle will then look a lot like a coil or strand of curly hair!
