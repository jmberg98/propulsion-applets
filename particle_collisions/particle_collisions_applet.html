Ionization & Recombination

Plasma Physics

The clearest difference between chemical rockets (think Space Shuttle main engines) and electric rockets (think Starlink Hall thrusters) is the link in propellant and energy. Chemical rocket propellant, such as liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen, has stored energy, released by forming chemical bonds through combustion. Electric rocket propellant, such as Xenon gas, doesn’t have any stored energy. This permits us to use external sources of energy to accelerate the propellant using electric and magnetic fields.

Particles are only accelerated by electric and magnetic fields if they are charged. Thus, we must separate our propellant particles into positively and negatively charged particles, also called ionization. The two primary methods of ionization used in electric thrusters are electron bombardment and radio-frequency (RF) excitation. Both use energy to cause a neutral atom to break into a positively charged ion and a negatively charged electron. Electron bombardment uses the kinetic energy of electrons to provide the required energy for a neutral atom to be ionized, also known as the ionization energy.

Guiding Question

Does ionization require or release energy?

Ionization

Sometimes, when a positively charged ion and a negatively charged electron come in contact, they recombine into a neutral atom, releasing energy in the form of light. This causes the characteristic glow of plasmas in fusion reactors and electric thrusters. This is also a loss mechanism, as we seek to maximize the number of ions in the ionization chamber, which can be used to produce useful thrust for our engine.

Guiding Question

Does recombination require or release energy?

Recombination

Now that we are more familiar with these particle processes, we can explore choices of propellant for our electric thrusters. Recall that atoms can be ionized multiple times, achieving charges +1, +2, or +3. We seek a low first ionization energy, the energy required to remove the first electron from the neutral atom. This minimizes the amount of energy to ionize our propellant so it can be accelerated. However, we seek a high second ionization energy, the energy required to remove another electron from the positively charged ion. That energy could have been used to ionize another neutral atom, rather than increasing the charge of the ion.

Conceptual Questions
Why do many conventional electric propulsion propellants have a high atomic mass? Wouldn’t this lead to a lower specific impulse, since the particle’s exhaust speed leaving the thruster would be lower?
This is true! However, consider that electric propulsion devices already achieve very high specific impulses (1000s of seconds, or 10s of km/s). The thrust of these devices is commonly measured in millinewtons, similar to the weight of a mosquito. An easy way to increase the thrust, without increasing the power, is to use a propellant with a higher atomic mass, like Xenon or Krypton.
Electromagnetic fields provide greater acceleration to particles with more charge, so why don’t we want particles with charge greater than 1?
While a particle with greater charge will be accelerated more by electromagnetic fields, the highly excited particle can cause greater erosion damage to the accelerating grids on contact, limiting the overall lifetime of the thruster. Additionally, the increase to exhaust velocity or specific impulse is not very large (see Electrostatic Acceleration applet). The energy is thus better used to ionize another neutral atom, raising the thrust and specific impulse of the device.
