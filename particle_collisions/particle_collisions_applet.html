Particle Collisions — Ionization & Recombination

Plasma Physics

A plasma is held in balance by two opposing single-particle events. In ionization, a fast electron slams into a neutral atom and knocks one of its electrons loose, creating an ion. In recombination, a free electron is recaptured by an ion, rebuilding a neutral atom and releasing the energy as a photon. Together these set how ionized a gas becomes.

Ionization

An electron must carry at least a certain amount of energy to ionize an atom. What sets that threshold?

Ionizing Collision — e⁻ + M → M⁺ + 2e⁻

Slide the electron energy below Ei and the collision turns elastic — the electron simply bounces off and the atom stays neutral. Above Ei, one electron becomes two and an ion is born.

Recombination

When a free electron recombines with an ion, where does the released binding energy go?

Recombination is far more likely for which electrons?

Radiative Recombination — M⁺ + e⁻ → M + hν

Recombination is the reverse of ionization: it converts an ion back into a neutral atom. In a thruster it is a loss channel — every recombination on a wall or in the beam is a propellant atom that never produced thrust.
