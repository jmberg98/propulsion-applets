Ionizing Collision

A single ionizing event: a fast electron strikes a neutral propellant atom. If it carries more than that atom's ionization energy Ei, it knocks a second electron loose — leaving a positive ion and two electrons. Below Ei it just bounces off and the atom stays neutral.

Radiative Recombination

The reverse of ionization: a free electron is captured by a positive ion, dropping into a bound orbit and rebuilding a neutral atom. The binding energy it gives up leaves as a photon hν. Recombination strongly favors slow electrons — a fast electron sails past the ion without being caught, scattering off its field instead.
