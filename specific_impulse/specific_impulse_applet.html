Thrust & Specific Impulse

In-Space

Propulsion systems expel propellant to generate thrust and ultimately change the momentum of the vehicle. Propulsion systems must be designed with key design constraints in mind. Engineers must consider mass budget, power budget, and satisfy the required mission time. These requirements dictate the exhaust velocity, input power, and thrust of the propulsion system respectively.

Conceptual Questions
Why do high thrust propulsion systems tend to be less mass efficient? Likewise, why do more mass-efficient systems typically have lower thrust?
There is a limit to the amount of input power available to accelerate the propellant, whether from chemical energy stored in the fuel or on-board solar/nuclear power. To achieve high thrust, a large mass flow rate of propellant must be accelerated, resulting in less power being delivered to each unit of propellant mass and thus a lower exhaust velocity and lower specific impulse. Conversely, to achieve high exhaust velocity and high specific impulse, the power must be distributed over a smaller mass flow rate, resulting in lower thrust.
How can high thrust and high specific impulse be achieved by the same propulsion system?
High thrust and high specific impulse requires a high power budget. There are drawbacks, however, to arbitrarily increasing input power. The amount of power that a power source can deliver scales with its mass. This can adversely affect the mass budget and mission time of the spacecraft. To effectively accomplish a higher power, higher power density is required. This makes nuclear power sources very appealing, as both fusion and fission can achieve much higher power densities than solar panels. Fission reactors have been flown in space, but fusion reactors (the holy grail) have yet to be realized.

Clarifying Points
Why do we care about mass efficiency? Can't we just carry more propellant or refuel?
In space, propellant is carried at a premium: every kg of propellant takes from the mass budget, leaving less mass available for payload and other important subsystems. Carrying extra propellant increases the mass of the vehicle, meaning the propulsion system must additionally handle the increased mass (more on this in the Ideal Rocket Equation!). Additionally, refueling introduces complexity and is not always possible, especially for deep-space missions.
Where does the input power for the propulsion system come from?
Ground-based propulsion systems exclusively rely on the stored chemical energy of rocket fuel and oxidizer for power to escape Earth's gravity. Once in space, other power sources become available. Spacecraft rely on solar or nuclear power to run their payloads — leftover energy can be used to drive an electric propulsion system. This simulation assumes that input power and mass flow rate are decoupled, which is true for electric propulsion. Traditional chemical engines rely on combustion, so input power and mass flow rate are inherently coupled.
Thrust

Let's derive the thrust of a general rocket propulsion device, which carries its own propellant. Apply conservation laws to a control volume. Assume steady-state operation, no viscous forces, and a uniform exit stream with density $\rho_e$, velocity $v_e$, area $A_e$, and exit pressure $P_e$, expelling into ambient pressure $P_{amb}$. Let $\hat{\mathbf{i}}$ denote the axial direction. The thrust stand measures the reaction force, i.e. the thrust, on the vehicle.

Conservation of Mass — at steady state there is no mass accumulation inside the control volume $$ \begin{aligned} 0 &= -\textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}} + \rho_e \textcolor{#00e5ff}{v_e} A_e \\[3pt] \Rightarrow\quad \textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}} &= \rho_e \textcolor{#00e5ff}{v_e} A_e \end{aligned} $$ Conservation of Momentum — Reynolds Transport Theorem for linear momentum $$ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\!\int_{CV}\!\rho\,\mathbf{V}\,dV \;+\; \oint_{CS}\!\rho\,\mathbf{V}\bigl(\mathbf{V}\cdot\hat{n}\bigr)\,dA \;=\; \sum\mathbf{F}_{ext} $$
Show detailed derivation — axial projection, steady-state reduction, force balance
Project onto the axial direction with a dot product against $\hat{\mathbf{i}}$ $$ \hat{\mathbf{i}}\cdot\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\!\int_{CV}\!\rho\,\mathbf{V}\,dV \;+\; \hat{\mathbf{i}}\cdot\oint_{CS}\!\rho\,\mathbf{V}\bigl(\mathbf{V}\cdot\hat{n}\bigr)\,dA \;=\; \hat{\mathbf{i}}\cdot\sum\mathbf{F}_{ext} $$ Steady state — the unsteady volume integral vanishes $$ \begin{aligned} \hat{\mathbf{i}}\cdot\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\!\int_{CV}\!\rho\,\mathbf{V}\,dV &= 0 \\[5pt] \Rightarrow\quad \hat{\mathbf{i}}\cdot\oint_{CS}\!\rho\,\mathbf{V}\bigl(\mathbf{V}\cdot\hat{n}\bigr)\,dA &= \hat{\mathbf{i}}\cdot\sum\mathbf{F}_{ext} \end{aligned} $$ Momentum flux term — only the exit plane contributes; propellant is initially at rest on the vehicle $$ \begin{aligned} \hat{\mathbf{i}}\cdot\oint_{CS}\!\rho\,\mathbf{V}\bigl(\mathbf{V}\cdot\hat{n}\bigr)\,dA &= \rho_e v_e^2 A_e \\ &= \bigl(\rho_e v_e A_e\bigr)v_e \\ &= \dot{m}\,v_e \end{aligned} $$ External forces — thrust-stand reaction plus net pressure force at the exit plane $$ \hat{\mathbf{i}}\cdot\sum\mathbf{F}_{ext} \;=\; F_N + \bigl(P_{amb} - P_e\bigr)A_e $$
Equate momentum flux and external forces, then isolate thrust $$ F_N + \bigl(P_{amb} - P_e\bigr)A_e = \dot{m}\,v_e $$ $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#ff4060}{F_N} \;=\; \textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{v_e} \;+\; \bigl(\textcolor{#69f0ae}{P_e - P_{amb}}\bigr)A_e } $$
Define effective exhaust velocity $c$ to absorb the pressure correction into a single quantity $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#00e5ff}{c} \;\equiv\; \textcolor{#00e5ff}{v_e} \;+\; \frac{\bigl(\textcolor{#69f0ae}{P_e - P_{amb}}\bigr)A_e}{\textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}} } \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \textcolor{#ff4060}{F_N} = \textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c} $$ Thrust = mass flow rate × effective exhaust velocity.
Specific Impulse & Input Power

A propulsion system is designed to give a change in momentum, or impulse, to the spacecraft. Recall that propellant is limited in space, so we normalize the impulse by propellant mass (or historically, propellant weight on Earth). In the spacecraft frame of reference, the expelled propellant flow begins at rest and is accelerated to leave with effective exhaust velocity $c$, so the propellant stream has jet power equivalent to input power times the power system's efficiency factor.

Total impulse — assume constant effective exhaust velocity during the burn $$ \begin{aligned} \text{Impulse} &= \int \textcolor{#ff4060}{F_N}\,dt = \int \textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}\,dt = \textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}\int \textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}\,dt = \textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{m_{\mathrm{prop}}} \end{aligned} $$ Normalize by propellant mass — impulse per unit propellant mass equals effective exhaust velocity $$ \frac{\text{Impulse}}{\textcolor{#00e5ff}{m_{\mathrm{prop}}}} = \textcolor{#00e5ff}{c} \qquad[\mathrm{m/s}] $$ Historical convention — divide by $g_0$ to obtain specific impulse in seconds $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#00e5ff}{I_{sp}} = \frac{\text{Impulse}}{\textcolor{#00e5ff}{m_{\mathrm{prop}}}\,g_0} = \frac{\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}}{g_0} \qquad[\mathrm{s}] } $$
Show jet power derivation — kinetic energy argument
Jet kinetic energy — in the spacecraft frame the propellant starts at rest, so all KE comes from the propulsion system $$ \begin{aligned} E_{\mathrm{jet}} &= \tfrac{1}{2}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{m_{\mathrm{prop}}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}^2 \\[5pt] P_{\mathrm{jet}} = \frac{dE_{\mathrm{jet}}}{dt} &= \tfrac{1}{2}\,\textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}^2 \end{aligned} $$ Use the thrust relation $F_N = \dot{m}\,c$ to rewrite jet power $$ P_{\mathrm{jet}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\textcolor{#ff4060}{F_N}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c} $$
Relate jet power to input power through system efficiency $\eta$ $$ \boxed{ P_{\mathrm{jet}} = \textcolor{#b388ff}{\eta\,P_{\mathrm{input}}} \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \tfrac{1}{2}\,\textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}^2 = \textcolor{#b388ff}{\eta\,P_{\mathrm{input}}} } $$ Solve for exhaust velocity, thrust, and specific impulse $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#00e5ff}{c} = \sqrt{\frac{2\,\textcolor{#b388ff}{\eta\,P_{\mathrm{input}}}}{\textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}}} } \qquad \boxed{ \textcolor{#ff4060}{F_N} = \textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c} = \sqrt{2\,\textcolor{#b388ff}{\eta\,P_{\mathrm{input}}}\,\textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}} } $$ $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#00e5ff}{I_{sp}} = \frac{1}{g_0} \sqrt{\frac{2\,\textcolor{#b388ff}{\eta\,P_{\mathrm{input}}}}{\textcolor{#ff4060}{\dot{m}}}} } $$ For fixed input power, lowering mass flow rate raises exhaust velocity / $I_{sp}$, but also reduces thrust. Designing a propulsion system around a power source entails balancing thrust–$I_{sp}$ trade.
