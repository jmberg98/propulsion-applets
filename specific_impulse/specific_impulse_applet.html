Thrust & Specific Impulse

In-Space

An electric thruster expels propellant to generate thrust. Two key metrics govern performance: thrust — the instantaneous force produced — and specific impulse ($I_{sp}$) — the impulse per unit propellant mass, the primary figure of merit for propellant efficiency.

Thrust — Control Volume Analysis

Apply conservation laws to a control volume (C.V.) enclosing the nozzle exit. Assume steady-state operation, no viscous forces, and a uniform exit stream at velocity $v_e$, exit area $A_e$, and pressure $P_e$, exhausting into ambient $P_{amb}$. The thruster stand exerts reaction force $F_N$ on the vehicle.

Conservation of Mass — net mass outflow = 0 at steady state $$0 = -\textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}} + \rho\,v_e\,A_e \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}} = \rho\,v_e\,A_e$$ Conservation of Momentum — Reynolds Transport Theorem, time-derivative vanishes at steady state $$\underbrace{\dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}\!\int_{CV}\!\rho\,\mathbf{V}\,dV}_{=\;0} \;+\; \oint_{CS}\rho\,\mathbf{V}\!\left(\mathbf{V}\cdot\hat{n}\right)dS \;=\; \sum\mathbf{F}_{ext}$$ Axial component — only the exit plane contributes momentum flux; pressure acts on all C.V. surfaces $$\textcolor{#c8d4e8}{F_N} \;=\; \underbrace{\textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{v_e}}_{\text{kinetic thrust}} \;+\; \underbrace{\textcolor{#69f0ae}{(P_e - P_{amb})\,A_e}}_{\text{pressure thrust}}$$
Define effective exhaust velocity $c$ — unifies kinetic and pressure contributions into one term $$\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c} \;\equiv\; \textcolor{#00e5ff}{v_e} + \frac{\textcolor{#69f0ae}{(P_e - P_{amb})\,A_e}}{\textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}}} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \boxed{\;\textcolor{#c8d4e8}{F_N} = \textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}}\cdot\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}\;}$$ Thrust = mass flow rate × effective exhaust velocity.  When $P_e = P_{amb}$, pressure terms cancel and $c = v_e$.
Specific Impulse & Input Power

Propellant is carried at a premium — every kilogram displaces payload. Specific impulse normalises total impulse by propellant mass (or Earth weight), making it the universal efficiency metric. In the spacecraft frame, expelled propellant carries kinetic energy, directly linking electrical input power to exhaust velocity.

Total impulse — integrate thrust over burn time for constant $c$ $$\text{Impulse} = \int \textcolor{#c8d4e8}{F_N}\,dt = \int \textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}\,dt = \textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}\int \textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}}\,dt = \textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}\cdot m_\text{prop}$$ Specific impulse — two equivalent conventions; seconds is historical, m/s is physical $$\textcolor{#00e5ff}{I_{sp}} = \frac{\text{Impulse}}{m_\text{prop}\,g_0} = \frac{\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}}{g_0}\;\;[\text{s}] \qquad\text{or}\qquad \textcolor{#00e5ff}{I_{sp}} = \frac{\text{Impulse}}{m_\text{prop}} = \textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}\;\;[\text{m/s}]$$
Jet power — kinetic energy delivered to exhaust per unit time in the spacecraft frame $$\textcolor{#b388ff}{P_{jet}} = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\,m_\text{prop}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}^2\right) = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\textcolor{#c8d4e8}{F_N}\cdot\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}$$ Couple to electrical input through efficiency $\eta$, then solve for $c$ and $F_N$ $$\textcolor{#b388ff}{P_{jet}} = \eta\,\textcolor{#b388ff}{P_{input}} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}}\,\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c}^2 = \eta\,\textcolor{#b388ff}{P_{input}}$$ Final results $$\boxed{\;\textcolor{#00e5ff}{c} = \sqrt{\dfrac{2\,\eta\,\textcolor{#b388ff}{P_{input}}}{\textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}}}}\;} \qquad\qquad \boxed{\;\textcolor{#c8d4e8}{F_N} = \sqrt{2\,\eta\,\textcolor{#b388ff}{P_{input}}\cdot\textcolor{#ff9100}{\dot{m}}}\;}$$ Higher input power or lower mass flow both raise exhaust velocity & $I_{sp}$ — but reducing $\dot{m}$ also reduces thrust.
