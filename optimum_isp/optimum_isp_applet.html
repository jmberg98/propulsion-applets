Optimal Specific Impulse & Mission Design

In-Space

Spacecraft and satellites are designed with mission requirements in mind, namely a mission Delta-V, desired payload mass, and required mission time. While technological and physical constraints restrict propulsion device choices, these mission requirements help guide what propulsion device is best for the desired mission. Conceptual Questions
Why isn't higher specific impulse always better? How does higher specific impulse affect mission time for a given power source?
Spacecraft propulsion systems must satisfy a desired mission Delta-V, a required mission time, and a desired payload mass. The best specific impulse is therefore not simply the highest one available, but the one that best satisfies the full set of mission requirements. If we arbitrarily increase specific impulse without increasing the technology level or efficiency of the power system, we must usually compromise somewhere else. For a fixed power source, higher specific impulse generally means lower thrust, which increases mission time. For example, a spacecraft powered by solar panels may achieve excellent propellant efficiency, but only by accepting lower thrust and therefore a longer mission time.
How does our mission Delta-V affect the optimum exhaust velocity and thus desired specific impulse?
As long as the required mission Delta-V is small compared with the effective exhaust velocity $c$, the Stuhlinger characteristic velocity is a good approximation to the optimum exhaust velocity. Lowering the required mission Delta-V, while holding other mission parameters fixed, favors lower thrust, higher specific impulse propulsion because more time is available to build up the required change in velocity. Raising the required mission Delta-V for the same mission time instead favors higher thrust, lower specific impulse propulsion, since less time is available for the power system to supply the needed energy. In that sense, higher mission Delta-V pushes the design toward shorter, more power-intensive burns, while lower mission Delta-V makes slower, more mass-efficient propulsion more attractive.
Clarifying Points
Why do we only care about optimizing for payload mass? Aren't there other design specifications that our spacecraft can be optimized for?
This is an important observation. In many missions, we seek to maximize payload mass so that the spacecraft can carry as much useful equipment or cargo as possible, such as telecommunications hardware, imaging instruments, telescopes, scientific packages, or crew supplies. However, some missions care more about other design objectives, such as shorter mission time, lower total cost, or greater reliability. The Stuhlinger curve is specifically useful for understanding how to maximize payload mass, but the underlying ideas and mathematics also provide a strong foundation for optimizing other mission parameters.
If the Stuhlinger characteristic velocity isn't the optimum exhaust velocity, what is its significance?
MIT's 16.522 course notes capture the meaning of $v_{ch}$ well: if all power source mass were accelerated by converting all available electrical energy during time $t_m$, it would reach $v_{ch}$, where $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{ch}} = \sqrt{\frac{2\,\textcolor{#C060FF}{\eta}\,t_m}{\textcolor{#C060FF}{\alpha}}} } $$ It is therefore effectively the speed limit of the propulsion system. A real spacecraft never reaches this speed, because some of its mass must remain as payload, structure, tanks, and other non-propellant components.
Delta-V & The Ideal Rocket Equation

The overall change in velocity of a spacecraft is determined by both the difference in its initial and final mass and by its specific impulse. While higher specific impulse most significantly increases the total Delta-V of the spacecraft, recall that higher specific impulses require higher total power to achieve the same mission time. However, higher total power is not trivial, as higher power requirements necessitate higher power + propulsion system mass or greater technology levels.

Ideal Rocket Equation $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\Delta v} = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}\ln\!\left(\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m_0}}{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m_f}}\right) } $$

Spacecraft Mass

The various subsystems of a spacecraft comprise its mass components, namely the structure, payload, propellant, and power source. Generally, the spacecraft is optimized for payload mass so that it may perform useful tasks. The structural mass is treated as fixed by the design, while the propellant and power source masses vary with the propulsion and power systems.

Objective Function & Stuhlinger Velocity

For fixed initial mass, structural mass, and mission time, we seek to maximize payload mass. This is equivalent to minimizing the combined propellant mass and power source mass with respect to exhaust velocity. The notes then use the Stuhlinger approximation for the case $\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\Delta v} \ll \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}$.

Stuhlinger Velocity $$ \boxed{ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{ch}} \equiv \sqrt{\frac{2\,\textcolor{#C060FF}{\eta}\,t_m}{\textcolor{#C060FF}{\alpha}}} } $$
Show Stuhlinger velocity derivation
Derivation

Seek to minimize mass of power + propulsion system w/ respect to c

$$ \left.\frac{d(\textcolor{#C060FF}{m_{ps}}+\textcolor{#FF4400}{m_p})}{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}}\right|_{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F_N},\textcolor{#C060FF}{\eta},t_m}=\frac{d}{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}}\left(\frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{\alpha} \textcolor{#FF4400}{F_N} \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}}{2\textcolor{#C060FF}{\eta}}+\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F_N}}{c}t_m\right)=0 $$ $$ \rightarrow \frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{\alpha} \textcolor{#FF4400}{F_N}}{2\textcolor{#C060FF}{\eta}}-\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F_N} t_m}{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}^2}=0 $$ $$ \rightarrow \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}^2=\frac{2\textcolor{#C060FF}{\eta} t_m}{\textcolor{#C060FF}{\alpha}} $$ $$ \rightarrow \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{ch}}\equiv\sqrt{\frac{2\textcolor{#C060FF}{\eta} t_m}{\textcolor{#C060FF}{\alpha}}} $$
Optimum Exhaust Velocity Objective $$ \boxed{\mathrm{Obj}=e^{-\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\Delta v}/\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}}-\frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{\alpha} \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}^2}{2\textcolor{#C060FF}{\eta} t_m}\left(1-e^{-\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\Delta v}/\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c}}\right)} $$ (Maximum at $\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{c_{opt}}$)
