Magnetic Mirror

Plasma Physics

We may be interested in trapping or guiding charged particles along paths of interest. To do this, we must implement magnetic mirrors, which trap particles that approach it at a specific angle. The “how” of magnetic mirrors lies in how charged particles exhibiting cyclotron motion generate a magnetic moment and act as a magnetic dipole (think of a bar magnet)! Explore the demos below to see how these orbiting charged particles act as bar magnets. Then explore how they function in non-uniform magnetic fields to see magnetic mirrors in action.

Guiding Question

If a charged particle is in motion, it induces a magnetic field. What is the shape of the induced magnetic field?

Induced Magnetic Field
Cyclotron & Larmor Motion

Recall that when a particle travels perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field, it travels circularly, undergoing cyclotron or Larmor motion. The charged particle’s circular motion induces a magnetic field.

Guiding Question

The induced magnetic field of a particle in circular motion can be summed to form a net magnetic field or moment. What is the shape of the net magnetic field or moment from a particle exhibiting cyclotron or Larmor motion?

Net Magnetic Moment
No Work & Constant Moment

Also recall that the magnetic force does no work on particles. The net kinetic energy of a charged particle in a magnetic field that does not change with time, is constant. Additionally, the magnetic moment of the particle is constant. By using a magnetic field that varies in strength in different regions, we can reduce the speed along the field lines by increasing the rotational speed around them.

Guiding Question

If a charged particle in cyclotron motion travels toward a stronger magnetic field, what happens to its motion?

Magnetic Mirror
Magnetic Moment

The net magnetic field or moment of a particle in cyclotron or Larmor motion is its magnetic moment. Its strength is determined by its current and the area of its circular path. It scales with the square of the particle’s perpendicular velocity and inversely scales with the strength of the magnetic field it is traveling about.

$$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu} = \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}^{2}}{2\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}} \;} $$
Show derivation
A moving charged particle carries current. Current induces a magnetic field about the particle’s path. To obtain the direction of $\vec{B}$, stick your thumb in the direction of travel if positively charged, or opposite direction if the particle is negatively charged. Then, curl your fingers to obtain $\vec{B}$’s clockwise or counterclockwise orientation. Maxwell–Ampere’s Law $$ \oint \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}}\cdot d\vec{s} = \mu_0 I + \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} $$ In a loop, the magnetic field induced by the current of the moving charged particle creates a net magnetic moment by vector sum of each induced magnetic field. For a particle in Larmor motion $$ \textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu} = \tfrac{1}{2}\int \vec{r}\times\vec{\jmath}\,dV = IA $$ $$ I = \frac{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\omega_c}{2\pi} \qquad A = \pi r_L^{2} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu} = \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}^{2}}{2\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}} $$
Invariant Magnetic Moment

Key to the derivation of magnetic mirrors, but very non-intuitive, is the invariance of the magnetic moment. The kinetic energy of the particle can be written as the sum of its parallel and perpendicular kinetic energy components. Then, it can be further rearranged to be the sum of a magnetic potential energy and the parallel component of the particle’s kinetic energy. Because the magnetic moment is constant, a varying magnetic field will affect the parallel velocity of the particle, allowing us to “speed up” and “slow down” the particle without adding or removing energy.

$$ \boxed{\; \frac{d\textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}}{dt} = 0 \;} $$
Show derivation
$$ \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}} = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel}\,\hat{k} + \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}\,\hat{\theta} \qquad \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel} = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v}\cos\theta_0 \qquad \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp} = \textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v}\sin\theta_0 $$ $$ \textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} = \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}\,\hat{k} + \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_r}\,\hat{r} $$ Magnetic Gauss’ Law $$ \nabla\cdot\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} = 0 $$ $$ \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial(r\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_r})}{\partial r} + \frac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}}{\partial z} = 0 $$ Assume $\dfrac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}}{\partial z}$ approximately constant (independent of $r$) $$ \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_r}) = -r\frac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}}{\partial z} $$ Integrate both sides w/ respect to r $$ \rightarrow\; r\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_r} = -\frac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}}{\partial z}\int_0^{r} r'\,dr' $$ $$ \rightarrow\; r\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_r} = -\frac{r^2}{2}\frac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}}{\partial z} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_r} = -\frac{r}{2}\frac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}}{\partial z} $$ Lorentz Force $$ \vec{\textcolor{#FF4400}{F}} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{\vec{v}}\times\textcolor{#00DDB5}{\vec{B}} $$ $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{F_\parallel} = \textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_r} $$ $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel}}{dt} = -\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\frac{r}{2}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}\frac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}}{\partial z} $$ If Larmor motion $$ r = r_L = \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}}{\textcolor{#C060FF}{q}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel}}{dt} = -\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}^{2}}{2\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}\frac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}}{\partial z} = -\textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}\frac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_z}}{\partial z} $$ Kinetic Energy $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{K} = \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v}^{2} = \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel}^{2}}{2} + \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}^{2}}{2} \equiv \text{constant} $$ (no work added or removed) $$ \frac{d\textcolor{#FF4400}{K}}{dt} = 0 = \textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel}\frac{d\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel}}{dt} + \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}^{2}}{2\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}\right) $$ $$ = -\textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}\frac{\partial \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}{\partial z}\frac{dz}{dt} + \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}\right) $$ $$ 0 = -\textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}\frac{d\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}{dt} + \frac{d\textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}}{dt}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B} + \frac{d\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}}{dt}\,\textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \frac{d\textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}}{dt} = 0 $$
Magnetic Mirror Derivation

Magnetic mirrors do not affect all particles equally. Whether a particle is or isn’t trapped by the magnetic mirror depends on the initial pitch angle of the traveling particle.

$$ \boxed{\; \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B} = \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}\csc^2\theta_0 \;} \qquad \boxed{\; \theta_0 = \arcsin\sqrt{\frac{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B}}} \;} \qquad \boxed{\; R = \frac{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_{max}}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}} \;} $$
Show derivation
Use our two constant quantities, kinetic energy $K$ & magnetic moment $\mu$. $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{K} = \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel}^{2} + \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}^{2} $$ $$ \textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu} = \tfrac{1}{2}\frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\perp}^{2}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B}} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \textcolor{#FF4400}{K} = \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_\parallel}^{2} + \textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B} $$ Because $K$ & $\mu$ are constant, changing $B$ will change $v_\parallel$. In fact, at a sufficient $B_B$, the particle will “stop” and be reflected backwards by the Lorentz force. Let’s see what particle trajectories are reflected by the magnetic mirror. $$ \textcolor{#FF4400}{K_0} = \textcolor{#FF4400}{K_f} \qquad \textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu} = \frac{\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\perp 0}}^{2}}{2\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}} $$ $$ \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\parallel 0}}^{2} + \textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0} = \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\parallel B}}^{2}}_{=\,0} + \textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}\,\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B} $$ $$ \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\parallel 0}}^{2} = \textcolor{#F5C518}{\mu}\!\left(\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B} - \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}\right) $$ $$ \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\parallel 0}}^{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}\textcolor{#FF4400}{m}\,\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\perp 0}}^{2}\!\left(\frac{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}} - 1\right) $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \left(\frac{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\parallel 0}}}{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\perp 0}}}\right)^{2} = \frac{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}} - 1 $$ Recall from the particle’s original trajectory: $\tan\theta_0 = \dfrac{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\perp 0}}}{\textcolor{#3DBFFF}{v_{\parallel 0}}}$ $$ \rightarrow\; \cot^2\theta_0 = \frac{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}} - 1 $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \frac{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}} = 1 + \cot^2\theta_0 = 1 + \frac{\cos^2\theta_0}{\sin^2\theta_0} $$ $$ \frac{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}} = \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta_0}\underbrace{\left(\sin^2\theta_0 + \cos^2\theta_0\right)}_{1} $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B} = \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}\csc^2\theta_0 $$ $$ \frac{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B}} = \sin^2\theta_0 $$ $$ \rightarrow\; \theta_0 = \arcsin\sqrt{\frac{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}}{\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_B}}} $$ To get the maximum $\theta_{max}$ for which a particle is not yet trapped, define a mirror ratio $R = \textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_{max}}/\textcolor{#00DDB5}{B_0}$ $$ \rightarrow\; \theta_{max} = \arcsin\sqrt{\frac{1}{R}} $$
Conceptual Questions
How does a spatially-varying magnetic field speed up or slow down a charged particle if it cannot do work?
The total kinetic energy of the charged particle remains constant. However, the parallel velocity component of the particle’s kinetic energy can be changed by changing the perpendicular velocity component of its kinetic energy. Thus, we are able to “speed up” or “slow down” the particle’s travel without adding or removing kinetic energy.
Why do we not allow for a time-varying magnetic field in the derivation of the magnetic mirror?
Though not explored in this applet, a time-varying magnetic field produces an electric field by Faraday’s Law. Electric fields do work on electrically charged particles. Because magnetic mirrors work without energy input, a time-varying magnetic field wouldn’t effectively represent the physics of how magnetic mirrors modify the motion of charged particles. It would appear as though changing a particle’s parallel velocity must be the result of removing energy from the charged particle, which is not necessarily true!
